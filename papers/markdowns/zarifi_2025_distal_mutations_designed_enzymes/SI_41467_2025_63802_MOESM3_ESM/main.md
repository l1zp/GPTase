# Distal mutations enhance catalysis in designed enzymes by facilitating substrate binding and product release

Corresponding Author: Professor Roberto Chica

This file contains all reviewer reports in order by version, followed by all author rebuttals in order by version.

Reviewer comments:

Reviewer #1 (Remarks to the Author)

This is an interesting work by Zarifi and coworkers, which couples biochemical, structural and computational characterization to explore the role of distal mutations in optimizing catalysis by three de novo designed Kemp eliminases (HG3, 1A53-2 and KE70). Kemp eliminases are an important model system for protein engineering studies, and there is increasing interest in exploiting distal mutations in protein engineering. Thus the work is timely and relevant. It is overall also solid and well-executed, and therefore I do not have any technical concerns on the execution of the manuscript. The paper is also well written and easy to follow. However, there are a number of issues the authors should address in a revised version of their manuscript, as outlined below.

Major issues:

1. The core conclusion of the work, as emphasized also in the title, is that distal mutations enhance catalysis by facilitating substrate binding and product release. This is based, in part, on simulations of unliganded enzymes (coupled with experimental characterization).

In several designed Kemp eliminases, including HG3 (Blomberg et al., Nature 2013, 503, 418) examined in the current work, KE07 (Hong et al., Nat. Commun. 2018, 9, 3800), repurposed $\beta$ -lactamases (Gutierrez-Rus et al., J. Am. Chem. Soc. In Press, DOI: 10.1021/jacs.4c09428) and even recent Kemp eliminases generated by the Fleishman lab through complete computational design (Listov et al., bioRxiv DOI: 10.1101/2025.01.04.631280), the substrate takes on multiple binding poses, and the key role of distal mutations is evolutionary conformational selection, optimizing for a preferred substrate binding mode (in the case of KE07 directed evolution gave rise to multiple novel active site configurations). In the case of KE70, Tawfik optimized this variant but couldn’t obtain meaningful structures with ligand or transition state bound, so there it is less clear what is happening in terms of substrate binding (Khersonsky et al., J. Mol. Biol., 2011, 407, 391), although the enrichment of productive enzyme conformational substates in KE70 is considered in the current work. In any case, given that there are at least 4 examples of this substrate binding mode optimization in the literature, including in HG3 which is also studied in this work, it is surprising that this was not considered or discussed in the current manuscript, as it is definitely important for the HG3 series, and likely to be an important factor for designed Kemp eliminases more broadly, and not unique to these specific examples listed above. (To be clear, the manuscript does consider enrichment of enzyme ensembles, but seems to overlook also enrichment of substrate conformational ensembles).

Minor issues:

1. Could the authors provide additional information on whether there is a specific structural path or network of interactions connecting the distal mutations to the regions exhibiting increased flexibility? In particular, are the distal mutations located along pathways that mechanically or dynamically influence the active-site loops, or are they positioned more independently without a direct coupling? Further clarification on whether these mutations participate in coordinated motion pathways, or whether their effects are more distributed or indirect, would greatly help in understanding the mechanistic basis for their contribution to the observed changes in conformational dynamics.

2. Additionally, it would be helpful if the authors could comment on whether the introduced Shell mutations in 1A53-Shell result in a shift toward a more hydrophobic or hydrophilic surface character compared to the Designed or Core variants. It is logical thinking that an increase hydrophobic surface patches often decrease solubility and increase aggregation but by clarifying that might support other researchers.

3. Finally, in a minor point related to Figure 5a, could the authors clarify whether the grey areas correspond to less populated conformational states?

# Reviewer #2

(Remarks to the Author)

This manuscript explores the impact of distal mutations (i.e. mutations remote from the active site), identified during directed evolution of designed Kemp Eliminase enzymes, on specific steps in the catalytic cycle of these enzymes. To this end, the authors produce product ‘core’ and ‘shell’ variants of these enzymes which contain only the active site mutations or the distal mutations, respectively. A series of structural, kinetic (including viscosity dependent kinetic measurements) and computational experiments are then performed on the designed, core, shell and evolved enzymes which shed light on the role of the remote mutations, in many cases showing that these remote mutations facilitate substrate binding and/or product release steps that are often neglected during typical computational enzyme design pipelines. Overall, the manuscript provides new and important insights into how remote mutations introduced during enzyme evolution impact catalysis, and highlight how modelling of substrate binding/product release steps will be important in the future to design enzymes with efficiencies rivalling natural systems. As a result, I am supportive of publication in Nature Communications, however there are several points that the authors should consider and address prior to publication:

The authors should be commended for the large body of data presented in the manuscript, describing the detailed structural and biochemical characterization of three different designed/evolved Kemp Eliminases. However, the large body of data, coupled with some of the complex kinetic arguments that the authors are trying to relay, makes the manuscript rather dense and challenging to read in places. In addition the authors do not always do a good job of explaining the significance of differences observed between different enzyme lineages, for example the opposite NBT binding trends observed between the core and evolved variants of the HG and IA53 lineages (even though all variants they show positive KSVE values, which the authors claim to be evidence of a (partially) rate limiting substrate binding step). I appreciate that the authors are presenting a large body data and some complex principles, however even with some background knowledge of enzyme kinetics I found the manuscript challenging to understand in places (some specific examples are presented below). If there are areas where the authors can simply/more clearly state their mechanistic rationale to highlight the key messages, then I think this would lead to an improved manuscript.

Specific Comments:

Page 9, lines 187-190, ‘The minimal impact of distal mutations….. suggests that they may play a more significant role in accelerating other catalytic cycle steps such as substrate binding and product release, which can be efficiency bottlenecks’: This statement should either be deleted or reworded. I found the statement confusing as at this stage of the manuscript as I do not believe there is any evidence presented that points to the authors suggestion (more solid evidence is subsequently presented).

Page 10, line 216-218: ‘These results demonstrate that distal mutations accelerate substrate binding….’ However, the binding kinetics were determined with with the transition state analogue 6NBT and not the substrate. Therefore, while I agree that the authors interpretations are most likely correct, they should qualify this statement to make clear that there are some assumptions made here.

Page 11, line 329 (and related to the point above): For the IA53 Core and evolved variants, KSVE experiments revealed a slope of 1 for kcat/KM (note that the HG3 series also show a positive KSVE slope). However, for these variants 6NBT binding was found to be slower in the more active evolved variant, which is the opposite trend observed with the HG3 series (above). The authors do not do a good job of explaining why the opposite trend in binding affinities is observed with the IA53 core and evolved variants. Is this because 6NBT is not a good substrate analogue for these experiments?

Data Presented in table S7, determination of koff and KD values for NBT: The methods section describes how kon was determined, however I was unable to find the methods describing how koff and KD were derived. This information should be added to the methods section and possibly a short statement in the main text at the appropriate point.

Page 11, lines 221-238: I found this discussion quite confusing and it was unclear how the authors arrived at their conclusion that distal mutations enhance both the chemical transformation and modulate substrate binding/product release.

Reviewer #4 (Remarks to the Author)

The manuscript entitled "Distal mutations enhance catalysis in designed enzymes by facilitating substrate binding and product release" by Zarifi et al, presents a thorough and conceptually important study that investigates the role of distal mutations in enzyme catalysis, focusing on their contribution to substrate binding and product release rather than just activesite chemistry. Using a powerful framework involving de novo Kemp eliminases and a rational separation of active-site

(“Core”) and distal (“Shell”) mutations derived from directed evolution, the authors combine a variety of biophysical and structural techniques including kinetic analyses, X-ray crystallography, ensemble refinement, kinetic solvent viscosity effects, stopped flow, and molecular dynamics simulations to dissect the contributions of these mutations. The manuscript goes beyond identifying effects to propose mechanisms: distal mutations modulate loop opening, bottleneck radius, and conformational ensembles in a way that enhances specific steps of the catalytic cycle. This mechanistic clarity is a significant advance.

The work is carefully executed and provides substantial insights into enzyme design and dynamics. It addresses a significant knowledge gap and presents a compelling case that optimizing active-site geometry alone is insufficient for achieving high catalytic efficiency, distal mutations that modulate structural dynamics are equally critical.

The manuscript is well-written overall. A few long sentences in the Results and Discussion sections could be simplified for clarity, but no major edits are necessary.

I support this manuscript for publication in Nature Communications after addressing one major revision and all minor points below.

# Major revision:

The current Figure 2 and its accompanying legend are dense and difficult to interpret, primarily due to unclear labeling, overly compact layouts, and insufficient visual distinction between compared structures. To improve clarity, the labeling of each panel should be revised to more clearly describe the comparison being shown. For instance, instead of cryptic labels like “Core $( + )$ 6NBT” and “Shell $( - )$ 6NBT,” it would be more informative to use descriptive phrases such as “HG3-Core bound to 6NBT” versus “HG3-Core apo” or “HG3-Core without 6NBT.” These labels should be consistently color-coded and presented clearly within each panel, using a uniform font size and placement for readability.

Visual clarity would also benefit from increasing the contrast between the overlaid structures. Bound and unbound states should be represented with distinctly different colors. The ligand 6NBT should be prominently highlighted and labeled in each panel to help anchor the viewer’s interpretation of the binding site.

The overall layout of the panels could also be rearranged to enhance the logical flow of information. For instance, instead of splitting panels only by comparison type (i.e., apo vs. bound in panel a, Core vs. Evolved/Designed in panel b), it may be clearer to arrange the figure by protein variant, with each column dedicated to a specific variant (e.g., HG3, 1A53, KE70) and subpanels showing the relevant structural comparisons. This would help the reader follow how each variant changes across different conditions or evolutionary stages.

Additionally, key residues should be directly labeled next to their sticks within the figure. This avoids the need for the viewer to infer residue identity from spatial positioning alone, which is particularly difficult given the density of the overlays. Finally, the figure legend should be rewritten for clarity and precision. It should explicitly explain what each panel compares, specify which structures contain ligands or buffer molecules (such as MES), and clearly describe any re-refinements or modeled-in features. Phrases like “Core $( + )$ 6NBT” and “Evolved $( + )$ 6NBT” should be avoided in the legend, replaced instead with full descriptions that align with the new, clearer figure labels. Mentioning which residues are shown (i.e., only major conformers) is helpful, but this should come after describing the key structural insights presented in the panels.

# Minor revisions:

• The introduction provides a strong rationale for studying distal residue contributions to enzyme catalysis and the use of de novo designed enzymes. However, it fails to clearly introduce Kemp eliminases, which are the model system used in this study. The authors must explicitly define what Kemp eliminases are and justify their selection. Including this information is critical to contextualize the study and help readers understand why Kemp eliminases are an appropriate and powerful system for investigating the role of distal mutations in different catalytic cycle steps.

• All systems examined are single-step Kemp eliminases. The authors should briefly address whether and how these findings might generalize to more complex enzymes with multi-step catalysis or allosteric regulation.

• The use of “Core” and “Shell” to describe mutational sets may unintentionally imply spatial layering (e.g., buried vs surface). Clarifying that these terms refer to proximity to the active site, not physical location within the protein, would be helpful in the Introduction.

• In KSVE and stopped-flow analyses, the authors infer product release as rate-limiting in some cases based on indirect reasoning. It would be helpful to more explicitly acknowledge the limitations of these inferences where direct measurement are lacking.

• Also, the discussion alludes to synergistic effects between Core and Shell mutations, but no formal test of additivity or epistasis is presented. A brief qualitative or semi-quantitative analysis would enhance mechanistic understanding.

# Reponse:

We thank the reviewer for this insightful and well-informed comment. We fully agree that evolutionary selection of productive substrate binding poses is a key mechanism observed in multiple Kemp eliminase systems, including HG3, KE07, and others.

In our work, we evaluated the impact of distal mutations on the substrate binding pose by solving high-resolution crystal structures of enzyme variants bound to the transition-state analogue 6-nitrobenzotriazole (6NBT). Given its structural similarity to the substrate, we used 6NBT binding as a proxy for substrate binding. We prefer this experimental approach over computational alternatives such as unconstrained molecular dynamics (MD) simulations for several reasons:

1) Direct observation of binding pose: Crystallography with a bound transition-state analogue enables direct visualization of ligand positioning in the active site, providing high-confidence structural data without the uncertainties and sampling limitations that can accompany molecular dynamics simulations (e.g., variations between replicas, timescale, initial conditions and system preparation, etc.).

2) Limitations of substrate-based MD simulations: We have previously shown that MD simulations using the substrate, rather than a transition-state analogue, often fail to capture the improved catalytic geometry of more evolved Kemp eliminase variants (Osuna et al., Acc. Chem. Res. 2015, 48: 1080–1089). For example, in the HG3 series, the most active mutants (HG3.14 and HG3.17) exhibited poorer substrate binding geometries than earlier, less active variants when molecular dynamics with the substrate were used. By contrast, the expected catalytic contacts observed in 6NBT-bound crystal structures were maintained in MD simulations involving the more polarized, transitionstate analogue.

3) Transition-state relevance: Since catalysis depends on stabilizing the transition state, using a transition-state analogue allowed us to assess whether distal mutations influence the binding pose most relevant to the chemical step, rather than the groundstate substrate binding pose.

Using these crystal structures, we were able to make clear, side-by-side structural comparisons between Designed vs. Shell and Core vs. Evolved variants, enabling us to assess the effects of distal mutations on binding poses. Across these comparisons, we did not observe substantial differences in 6NBT binding pose attributable to distal mutations (Figure 2b), suggesting that distal mutations do not significantly alter the substrate binding mode in these enzymes.

To evaluate enrichment of substrate conformational ensembles, we performed ensemble refinement, which involves a molecular dynamics simulation constrained by the crystallographic electron density. This analysis revealed some conformational heterogeneity of the bound 6NBT (Figure 3), but the variation was modest and did not indicate a shift in the preferred binding pose due to distal mutations. This contrasts with previous crystallographic observations in systems like HG2 (Privett et al., PNAS 2012, 109: 3790–3795; PDB ID: 3NYD) and KE07 (Hong et al., Nature Communications 2018, 11: 5975; PDB ID: 5D2W vs. 6DNJ), where alternate ligand binding poses determined by X-ray crystallography were either eliminated by specific mutations (e.g., S265T in HG2 to produce HG3) or altered during evolution due to active-site remodeling.

To quantify the enrichment of substrate conformational ensembles, we have now measured angles defining the contact between the catalytic base and 6NBT in these ensembles, in an approach inspired by that described by Blomberg et al. (Nature 2013, 503: 418–421). These data, presented in new Supplementary Figure 8 (see below), show that distal mutations generally enhance the substrate binding ensemble by shifting angles of catalytic interactions toward their ideal values.

To do so, we performed ensemble refinement on the 6NBT-bound structures (Supplementary Table 6), using molecular dynamics constrained by electron density to generate conformations consistent with the experimental data. For both 1A53-Core and 1A53-Evolved, KSVE experiments on $k _ { c a t } / \kappa _ { M }$ revealed a slope of approximately 1.0 (Figure 4c), indicating that formation of productive enzyme-substrate complexes is much slower than the chemical transformation in both variants.
